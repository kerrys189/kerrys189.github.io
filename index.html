Three Decades of Woodland Change in Wedza, Zimbabwe

Land Cover is intricately linked to livelihoods and sustainable management of natural resources.

To understand how land cover is changing we used Landsat data between 1990 and 2020.

Why Wedza?

This section will contain information about the decision to choose Wedza.

Woodland cover has transitioned from net deforestation to net expansion.

But...

Woodland has not regrown in the same place it was lost from.

  • Area of woodland within 3km of households has increased
  • Distance to nearest woodland path (over 0.5ha) has decreased

Does this match local perceptions?

We interviewed eleven key informants who were researchers, farmers, teachers, and village heads.

The results did not match the findings from remote sensing.

10 out of 11 said access to woodland resources were limiting.

and

9 out of 11 said access to woodland resources was decreasing.

Why do we see this pattern?

Interview participants said that environmental change, government inputs, farm resettlement, increasing in mining, and changing farming practices played a part in land cover change. Tobacco farming was especially linked to woodland loss in the resettlement area.

Notably, there was no evidence from interviews that scarcity of woodland resources has led to replanting of trees...

... but there was evidence that off-farm employment was important in reducing pressure on the land, enabling woodland regrowth.
